How TurboQuant works — in pictures

TurboQuant is a KV-cache compression trick from Google Research (ICLR 2026). The core idea is two steps: rotate the cached K/V vectors by a random orthogonal matrix, then quantize each coordinate to very few bits. This page shows why the rotation matters.

1. Why naive quantization hurts

A transformer's K/V cache has outlier channels — a few dimensions with far larger values than the rest. When you uniformly quantize across the range of each channel, almost all the bits get spent on the outliers, and the "normal" values collapse into a few coarse buckets.

Per-channel range (max − min) across 1,000 simulated K vectors, dim = 16

Channels 3 and 11 have ~8× the range of the others — classic outlier pattern. At 2 bits (4 levels), their quantization step covers a huge gap; fine-grained information in the other 14 channels is lost. This is the problem TurboQuant solves.

2. Random rotation flattens the distribution

Multiply the same data by a random orthogonal matrix R (generated once, same for all vectors). Because R preserves dot products, attention math works the same on rotated K,V — the model doesn't notice. But the per-channel statistics change dramatically: outlier energy gets spread evenly across dimensions.

After rotation, all channels have roughly the same range — the outlier energy is spread across dimensions. Quantization can now use its bit budget on all channels equally, not just the outlier ones.

3. The bit-budget slider

At N bits we have 2N levels. A smooth signal becomes a staircase. More bits = finer steps = smaller reconstruction error. TurboQuant uses a Beta-distribution-optimal codebook (rather than uniform) because rotated values follow Beta((d−1)/2, (d−1)/2) on [-1, 1] — concentrated near zero.

This matches what we see in the real experiments. On Llama-3.1-8B at 2-bit, TurboQuant scores PPL 6.13 vs KIVI's naive 2-bit at 7.86. On Parler-TTS, skipping the rotation jumps WER from 0.21 to 0.65. The rotation is doing the heavy lifting.

5. The residual buffer

One extra trick: the most recent 128 tokens stay at full precision, only older tokens are quantized. Attention weights concentrate on recent context, so keeping those exact protects the most important information. The residual buffer is why short clips (~3 s of audio, < 128 tokens) look nearly identical at every bit level in our TTS benchmark — they never actually get quantized.

Code

The whole algorithm is ~150 lines of PyTorch, fully commented, in this repo: handrolled_turboquant.py. 

# The whole quantize step
x_f32 = x.float()
norms = x_f32.norm(dim=-1, keepdim=True)     # store length
x_unit = x_f32 / (norms + 1e-10)              # unit sphere
x_rotated = x_unit @ R.float()                # random rotation (the trick)
diffs = x_rotated.unsqueeze(-1) - self.codebook.float()
indices = diffs.abs().argmin(dim=-1).to(torch.uint8)  # quantize: nearest codebook entry
# To reconstruct: look up centroid, rotate back, rescale by norm
x_rotated_approx = self.codebook[indices.long()].float()
x_unit_approx = x_rotated_approx @ R.float().T
x_approx = x_unit_approx * norms
